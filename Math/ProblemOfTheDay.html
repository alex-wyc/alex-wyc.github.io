2015-01-05

Random Easy Problem: Prove DeMoivre's Theorem with induction.

If you didn't know (shame on you), DeMoivre's Theorem states the following: \[ (R cis(\theta))^n = R^n cis(\theta \times n) \]

Hint: ... Seriously? You can do this!

Solution:

Ok this should not be hard. Base case, \(n = 1\), kinda duh, just the original statement.

Inductive hypothesis, DeMoivre's Theorem holds for the \(n = k\), where \(k\) is some integer. So we get \((R cis(\theta))^k = R^k cis(\theta \times k)\).

Now multiply both sides by \(R cis(\theta)\), we get \[ (R cis(\theta))^{k+1} = R^{k+1} (\cos{\theta \times k} + i \sin{\theta \times k})(\cos{\theta} + i \sin{\theta}) \] Expand the right hand side, isolate the real and imaginary, we get that the left hand side equals: \[ R^{k+1}[(\cos{\theta \times k} \cos{\theta} - \sin{\theta\times k}\sin{\theta}) + i (\sin{\theta \times k}\cos{\theta} + \cos{\theta \times k}\cos{\theta})] \] Now use the angle addition formula, we get the final result: \[ \boxed{(R cis(\theta))^{k+1} = R^{k+1} cis(\theta \times (k+1))} \blacksquare \]

2015-01-03

Geometry Problem: If \(a, b, c\) are the sides of the triangle \(ABC\), \(t_a, t_b, t_c\) are the length of the angle bisectors of angles \(A, B, C\) and \(T_a, T_b, T_c\) are length of the extensions of \(t_a, t_b, t_c\) to the intersection of the circumcircle. Prove that: \[ abc = \sqrt{T_a T_b T_c t_a t_b t_c} \]

Hint: Prove \(ab = T_c t_c\).

Solution:

The hint is an important one, if we can proove the hint, we can easily get \(a^2 b^2 c^2 = T_a t_a T_b t_b T_c t_c\) and then we simply square root both sides. Therefore let's try to proove the hint.

Just a note, in the diagram and the following proof, I used \(P_x\) and \(Q_x\) to denote the foot of the point of intersection and the foot of the angle bisector.

Refer to the diagram above, if we construct \(\overline{AP_c}\) and \(\overline{BP_c}\). Now we have a cyclic quadrilateral. Since same arcs subtend same angles, we know that \(\measuredangle ACP_c \cong \measuredangle ABP_c\), we also know that \(\measuredangle CAB \cong \measuredangle CP_cB\).

By AA similarity, we know that \(\triangle BCP_c \sim \triangle Q_cAC\). Then we get the following ratio: \(\frac{b}{T_c} = \frac{t_c}{a}\). Cross multiply and we get \(ab = T_c t_c\).

Now repeat the process for all three angles and we'll get \(a^2 b^2 c^2 = T_a t_a T_b t_b T_c t_c\). Now we square root both sides and we get \(\boxed{abc = \sqrt{T_a T_b T_c t_a t_b t_c}} \blacksquare\)

2015-01-02

Algebra Problem: If \(x_1, x_2, x_3 ... x_n\) are positive, find with proof the minimum value of: \[ (x_1 + x_2 + x_3 + ... + x_n)(\frac{1}{x_1} + \frac{1}{x_2} + \frac{1}{x_3} + ... + \frac{1}{x_n}) \]

Problem from "Math Circle for Olympians" by Henryk Pawlowski

Hint: Use Rearrangement

Solution:

WLOG, we can assume that \(x_1 \geq x_2 \geq x_3 \geq ... \geq x_n\). Therefore, we also know that \(\frac{1}{x_n} \geq \frac{1}{x_{n-1}} \geq \frac{1}{x_{n-2}} \geq ... \geq \frac{1}{x_1}\).

From the rearrangement inequality, we know that the minimum value of any linear combination of these two sequences is when the order of the two sequences completely mismatches. Therefore the minimum value of any linear combination is \(\frac{1}{x_n} \cdot x_n + \frac{1}{x_{n-1}} \cdot x_{n-1} + \frac{1}{x_{n-2}} \cdot x_{n-2} + ... + \frac{1}{x_2} \cdot x_2 + \frac{1}{x_1} \cdot x_1\), which will give you n.

Now let's expand the left hand side: \[x_1 \cdot \frac{1}{x_1} + x_2 \cdot \frac{1}{x_2} + ... + x_n \cdot \frac{1}{x_n} +\] \[x_1 \cdot \frac{1}{x_2} + x_2 \cdot \frac{1}{x_3} + ... + x_n \cdot \frac{1}{x_1} +\] \[... ... ... ... ... ... ... ... ... ... ... ...\] \[x_1 \cdot \frac{1}{x_{n-1}} + x_2 \cdot \frac{1}{x_n} + ... + x_n \cdot \frac{1}{x_{n-2}} +\] \[x_1 \cdot \frac{1}{x_n} + x_2 \cdot \frac{1}{x_1} + ... + x_n \cdot \frac{1}{x_{n-1}}\]

Now we apply rearrangement to each "row" of the expansion. Notice hat each row is greater than or equal to n, and since there are n rows, their sum is greater than or equal to \(n \times n\), or \(\boxed{n^2}\blacksquare\)


Bonus Solution by Brandon Lin:

We create two vectors, \(\langle \sqrt{x_1}, \sqrt{x_2}, \sqrt{x_3}, ... , \sqrt{x_n} \rangle\) and \(\langle \frac{1}{\sqrt{x_1}}, \frac{1}{\sqrt{x_2}}, \frac{1}{\sqrt{x_3}}, ... , \frac{1}{\sqrt{x_n}} \rangle\).

Now we apply the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality, we get the following: \[ (x_1 + x_2 + x_3 + ... + x_n)(\frac{1}{x_1} + \frac{1}{x_2} + \frac{1}{x_3} + ... + \frac{1}{x_n}) \geq \] \[ (\frac{1}{\sqrt{x_1}} \cdot \sqrt{x_1} + \frac{1}{\sqrt{x_2}} \cdot \sqrt{x_2} + \frac{1}{\sqrt{x_3}} \cdot \sqrt{x_3} + ... + \frac{1}{\sqrt{x_n}} \cdot \sqrt{x_n})^2 \]

The right hand side simplifies to \(\boxed{n^2}\blacksquare\)
