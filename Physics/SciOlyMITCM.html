Problem 6 (6 pts)

Many odern vinyl turntables are belt drive; a motor with a small pulley is coupled to a much larger record platter with a belt. For the audio to have the right tempo and pitch, it's critical that the platter spins at \(33\frac{1}{3}\) rpm (for most records).

Suppose a turntable has a platter pulley radius of 12.0 cm and its drive pulley has a radius of 1.00 cm. Calculate:

  1. The speed in rpm that the motor must rotate at.
  2. The mechanical advantage of the belt system.
  3. The required torque of the motor given that the belt must apply 2.00N of force to the platter for it to spin at a constant \(33\frac{1}{3}\) rpm.

Solution:

Part 1:

It is crucial to find out what is being conserved here, and the quantity I used was translational displacement along the belt. Given a set amount of time, say 1 minute, the distance covered by the rotation of the platter must be covered by the rotation of the motor drive. So we first find the translational displacement (\(d_T\)):

\[ d_T = 2 \pi (12.0 cm) \times \frac{100}{3} rpm \times 1 min \approx 2510 cm \]

We know that the motor drive, rotating at \(\omega_m\) need to cover 2510 cm within 1 minute, so we get the following equation:

\[ 2510 cm = 2 \pi (1.00 cm) \times \omega_m \times 1 min \] \[ \boxed{\omega_m = 400. rpm} \]

Part 2:

This solution assumes that you do not know the formula for the IMA of a belt system. If you do the problem becomes trivial.

We need to figure out a formula for the IMA of a belt system, the process is quite simple really. Since the work done by both are the same, the torque applied must be equal, so we get:

\[ R_e \times F_e = R_r \times F_r \]

So we get that:

\[ IMA = \frac{F_r}{F_e} = \frac{R_r}{R_e} = \frac{12.0 cm}{1.00 cm} = \boxed{12} \]

Part 3:

This is just straight forward calculation:

\[ \tau = F \times R = 2.00N (1.00 cm) = \boxed{0.0200 Nm} \]

Problem 7 (7 pts)

To hold guitar strings at the right tension, guitar tuning machines uses a compound machine with a spur gear, a worm gear, and a wheel/axle (the knob). A particular tuning machine has a string peg radius of 3.00 mm (attached to the spur gear), a spur gear with 14 teeth and a knob radius of 9.00 mm.

  1. Calculate the gear ratio of the worm gear system.
  2. When the string is tuned with 3.84 N of force on the ends of the knob, the string's tension is 100. N. Calculate the efficiency of the tuning machine system.
  3. Suppose a string is tune at a tension of 80.0 N and you apply a lubricant that increases the efficiency found above by 0.200. Calculate the force in newtons you must apply to the ends of the knob.

Solution:

Side note: Unlike the last question, I was not able to do this question without looking stuff up...

Part 1:

So what is a gear ratio? A gear ratio is the amount by which the gear slows down the rotation of the system. For a worm gear, the ratio is teeth count : 1, so the ratio of this worm gear is \(\boxed{14:1}\).

Part 2:

The problem basically gives us the AMA of the system, which is \(\frac{100. N}{3.84 N} = 26.0\). However, in order to find the efficiency, we need to know the IMA.

The wheel and axel part has the IMA of 3 (\(\frac{9.00 mm}{3.00 mm}\)), and the worm-drive has mechanical advantage of 14, so the IMA is 42. Therefore, the efficiency is:

\[ \boxed{\frac{26}{42} = 0.620} \]

Part 3

So now the efficiency is 0.820, and the output force you want is 80.0 N. Since the IMA is 42 and the efficiency is 0.820, the AMA is 34.44, so the input force needed is \(\boxed{2.32 N}\)

Problem 8 (7 pts)

Your Bungee Drop teammates need help! They ask you to devise a clamp to anchor their bungee device that can be hand-tightened to the correct pressure for maximum convenience. They've determined that the clamp must exert 210. N of force on the bungee device to hold it and request that it can be tightened with only 5.00 N of effort. The clamp will use a lever coupled to a screw, similar to many vises. The only screw available for the clamp has an outside diameter of 12.0mm and a pitch of 2.0mm. Calculate the minimum lever length needed to satisfy their requirements.

Solution:

So bascially, your team mate want a wheel-axel-screen system that will amplify a 5.00 N force to a 210. N force. So the IMA of said machine is 42.0

The screw part of the system has a mechanical advantage of \(\frac{(12.0 mm) \pi}{2.00 mm} = 18.8\). This means that the wheel-axel system need to have an IMA of \(42.0 \div 18.8 = 2.23\) to make this work.

So, we just have to solve for \(R_w\):

\[ \frac{R_w}{12.0mm \div 2} = 2.23 \] \[ R_w = 2.23 \times 6.00 mm = \boxed{13.4 mm} \]

Problem 9 (7 pts)

Nail clippers can be represented as a compound machine of two levers and a wedge that cleaves the fingernail.

  1. Using the specifications found in the diagram, calculate the mechanical advantage of each simple machine and also the entire compound machine.
  2. Assuming it takes 25.0 Newtons of lateral force on a fingernail to cleave it, how much effort must a user apply to cut their fingernails?

Solution:

Part 1:

  • Top Lever: easy class 2 lever, \(d_R = 15\)mm, \(d_E = 60\)mm, IMA = \(\frac{d_E}{d_R} = \boxed{4}\)
  • Bottom Lever: easy class 3 lever, \(d_R = 70\)mm, \(d_E = 50\)mm, IMA = \(\frac{d_E}{d_R} = \boxed{0.714}\)
  • Wedge: Same stuff, plug and chug, IMA = \(\frac{h}{w} = \frac{5.00 mm} {2.00 mm} = \boxed{2.5}\)
  • Total: 7.1, just multiply everything together.

Part 2:

So you know that the IMA of the entire thing is 7.1, and the output force is 25.0 N, one simply divide to get the effort, which is \(\boxed{3.5 N}\)

Problem 10 (7 pts)

To lower the load on the motor in a traditional elevator, a counterweight is used on one end of a pulley system to balance with the elevator cab.

For the elevator pulley system pictured, the elevator cab has a mass of 500. kg and must have a passenger capacity of \(2.00 \times 10^3\) kg.

  1. Find a counterweight mass that minimizes the motor load at half the passenger capacity.
  2. For a list motor with 10.0 cm pulley radius, what is the maximum torque required to allow static equilibrium at all capacities?

Solution

Part 1:

So the thing here is that the effort force on the movable pulley is actually the weight of the elevator, and the resistance is the weight of the counterweight. To minimize the motor drive, we want these two to sum up to zero, so we want the weight divided by two to equal to 1500 kg, so the weight must be \(\boxed{3000 kg}\)

Part 2

So I guess we keep the counterweight at 3000 kg, that cancls out 1500 of the 2500 kg that is the full capacity of the lift, so the motor has to lift 1000 kg of material, to find the torque, we simply multiply:

\[ \tau = 1000 kg \times 9.81 \frac{m}{s^2} \times 0.1 m = \boxed{98.1 Nm} \]

Problem 11 (8 pts)

In construction cranes, the main horizontal jib must be counter-weighted on the opposite side of the load so that the crane doesn't collapse at the vertical support point. You are designing a crane that must be able to lift a \(5.00 \times 10^3\) kg load 70.0 meters away from the vertical support. Hpwever, the vertical support can withstand \(2.00 \times 10^6\) Newton meters of torque before failing. Use 9.81 as the acceleration of gravity, g.

  1. What is an approporiate counterweight mass needed if the counterweight is mounted 20.0m behind the vertical support? The crane must not collpase under no load as well.
  2. Using the counterweight mass found above, what is the maximum load mass the crane can support 20.0 m in front of the vertical support?

Solution:

Part 1:

On problems like this we look at the extremes -- maximum weight and no weight. Let's first look at no weight.

If there's no weight, then the only torque experienced by the vertical support point is from the counterweight, so:

\[ m(9.81\frac{m}{s^2})(20.0 m) \lt (2.00 \times 10^6 Nm) \] \[ m \lt (1.02 \times 10^4 kg) \]

Now let's look at the other extreme, if the mass is \(5.00 \times 10^3\) kg at the end of the crane:

\[ (5.00 \times 10^3 kg)(9.81 \frac{m}{s^2})(70.0 m) - M(9.81 \frac{m}{s^2})(20.0 m) \lt (2.00 \times 10^6 Nm) \] \[ M \gt (7.31 \times 10^3 kg) \]

So anything between m and M works.

Part 2:

Maximum load mass means the \(\tau_L - \tau_C = 2.00 \times 10^6 Nm\), so let's find that:

\[ m(9.81\frac{m}{s^2})(20.0 m) - (M_C)(9.81\frac{m}{s^2})(20.0 m) = 2.00 \times 10^6 Nm \] \[ m-M_C = 1.01 \times 10^4 kg \] \[ \boxed{m = M_c + 1.01 \times 10^4 kg} \]
